Assistant: Given dramatic winter scenery without skiing, and a February window, here are three directions worth considering:

Iceland -- The classic choice for February. Glacier hikes, black sand beaches, ice caves (only accessible in winter), and the best conditions for northern lights. Faroe Islands -- Less visited than Iceland, dramatically rugged, February is genuinely remote and quiet. Grass-roofed villages, sea cliffs, and almost no crowds. Worth considering if you want somewhere that feels truly off the beaten path.

Norwegian fjords (Bergen or Flam area) -- Snow-covered fjords in February are striking. The Flam Railway is one of the most scenic train routes in Europe and runs year-round. Bergen has a compact, walkable old town. Prices are higher than Iceland but the infrastructure is excellent.

Assistant: Iceland in February breaks down well for a week:

What works well in February:
- Ice caves inside Vatnajokull glacier -- these are only accessible November to March before they melt. They are genuinely impressive and most day tours depart from Jokulsarlon.
- Golden Circle (Thingvellir, Geysir, Gullfoss waterfall) -- all accessible by day tour from Reykjavik, full-day.
- Reykjanes Peninsula -- volcanic landscape, geothermal fields, accessible year-round. The peninsula became more active after recent eruptions, so check current status before visiting lava fields.
- Reykjavik itself -- compact and walkable. Hallgrimskirkja church for the views, Harpa concert hall on the waterfront, the old harbour area.
- Northern lights -- February gives you 14+ hours of darkness. No guarantees (cloud cover is the main variable), but your odds are reasonable over a week.

Practical notes:
- A rental car is the most flexible option if you are comfortable driving in winter conditions (studded tyres are standard). Otherwise, day tours from Reykjavik cover most highlights.
- February temperatures hover around 0-3C in Reykjavik, colder inland. Waterproof outer layers and thermal base layers are essential.
- Book ice cave tours well in advance -- they sell out in February.

You: What about food? What should I eat?

Assistant: Icelandic food worth trying:

Traditional:
- Lamb -- Iceland has free-range lamb that is genuinely distinctive. It appears on most menus and is usually the best value way to eat well.
- Skyr -- thick Icelandic dairy product, somewhere between yogurt and soft cheese. Every supermarket sells it, good for breakfast.
- Plokkfiskur -- Icelandic fish stew, a traditional working-class dish, warming and good in winter.
- Kleinur -- twisted fried dough pastry, a classic Icelandic snack available at bakeries.

Worth knowing:
- Reykjavik has a good restaurant scene for a city its size, with strong fish and lamb options. Prices are high by most standards -- budget 3,000-5,000 ISK (~20-35 EUR) per main at a mid-range spot.
- Supermarkets (Bonus, Kronan) are significantly cheaper for breakfast and lunch supplies.
- The hot dog stands (Pylsur) are an Icelandic institution and genuinely good -- the classic order is with everything (remoulade, raw onion, crispy onion, ketchup, mustard).

I cannot name specific restaurants without live data, but searching for Reykjavik restaurants by neighbourhood (the old harbour and Laugavegur street area have the highest concentration) will give you current options.
